# d'ami — Visual DNA

## Core Identity
d'ami is California chic with a romantic European soul. Our world is warm, effortless, and quietly elevated — the kind of life lived in linen and good light. Every image should feel like a stolen moment from someone's best Saturday: wine at a Sonoma winery, flowers from the farmer's market, a walk to nowhere in particular. Unhurried, sun-touched, real.

## The World (Setting)
The d'ami universe lives between the California outdoors and a well-loved home. Think outdoor winery tables on concrete patios, farmer's market sidewalks in warm light, marble kitchen counters with window views into a garden, and clean stucco walls. It is modern and lived-in simultaneously — not a museum, not a farmhouse.

- **Outdoor settings:** Winery terrace with stone or concrete tables, farmer's market sidewalk, outdoor patio with linen shade, sun-dappled street, garden table under an olive tree
- **Indoor settings:** Marble kitchen counter, white-painted interior with warm natural light, linen-covered dining table, clean modern bathroom with marble

## Lighting & Atmosphere
- **Primary rule:** Warm, bright California diffused light — the quality of a late-morning sun through linen curtains, or an overcast coastal day. Crisp but never cold. Whites appear warm-white or ivory, never blue-grey.
- Not golden hour (too orange), not harsh midday sun (too flat). The sweet spot is a bright, naturally lit scene with soft warmth.
- Shadows are soft, warm, and gentle. The scene should feel sun-touched and alive, like a warm morning.
- Outdoors: dappled light through olive trees or a market awning. Concrete and stone surfaces catch and diffuse the light beautifully.

## Color Grading & Film Aesthetic
- Emulate a blend of Fuji Pro 400H and Kodak Portra 400: the warmth lives in the **materials** (cream fabric, ivory tones, warm skin) — NOT in the color grade itself. The color grade is neutral-to-slightly-cool. Whites are crisp. Fabric reads as its true color, not warmed or yellowed by a filter.
- **Base palette:** Ivory, cream, warm white linen — the warmth comes from the fabric, not the light color temperature.
- **Accent palette:** Dusty olive, navy, sage green, fresh pink, dusty blue — introduced through props, flowers, and textiles.
- Highlights: clean and slightly creamy. Shadows: soft, neutral — never warm-orange, never cold-blue.
- The rule: if the whites of a shirt or canvas bag look cream-yellow or orange-tinted, it is too warm. They should read as true white or true ivory. Fine grain present.

## Camera & Composition
- Standard focal lengths: 35mm–50mm. Natural, intimate, personal.
- Eye-level or slight overhead (flat lay for product moments). Feels like the viewer is present.
- Shallow depth of field (f/1.8–f/4): subject is sharp, background softly dissolves.
- Composition: candid, unstudied, found moments. Something half out of frame. A hand reaching in. A person mid-motion. Negative space matters.

## Fabrics & Materials (Critical)
- **Correct fabrics:** Stonewashed linen, worn denim, crisp white cotton, canvas (clean, structured), fine cotton voile, ivory relaxed knit worn casually
- **Wrong fabrics:** Thick rustic burlap, heavy artisan wool as product focus, synthetic fabrics
- **The product:** A clean canvas boat-and-tote — cream body, navy straps. Structured, functional, beautiful. Not precious.

## Props & Surfaces (Critical — derived from moodboard)
- **Correct surfaces:** White marble, stone or teak outdoor table tops, white-painted interior walls, warm wood accents (modern, not rustic)
- **Correct ground/floor surfaces (outdoors):** Lush green grass, fine gravel path, aged stone tile or terracotta pavers, weathered mulch, dry garden path. Never bare concrete or asphalt.
- **Correct floor surfaces (indoors):** Aged stone tile, terracotta hex tile, wide-plank painted wood, marble
- **Correct props (use these):**
  - Large-bowled wine glasses with sauvignon blanc or white wine
  - Artisan sourdough bread on a simple white plate with olive oil
  - Pink scabiosa, garden roses, sweet peas, loose wildflower stems
  - Sculptural glass or ceramic vases
  - Fresh market produce — strawberries, figs, lemons, heirloom tomatoes
  - Jo Malone-style fragrance bottles, small luxury objects
  - Linen napkins, simple white ceramic plates
  - The monogrammed boat-and-tote (cream/navy)
- **Wrong props:** Dried flowers, thread spools, embroidery hoops, very ornate silver pieces, old-world antiques

## Embroidery Style (Critical — defines the brand)
The embroidery is personal, narrative, and slightly playful — like a note slipped into a pocket. Every piece tells a small story about a person, a place, or a moment. It rewards the person who looks closely.

### Text & Lettering
- **Style:** Looks genuinely handwritten — slightly imperfect, flowing cursive. Like someone wrote a name or phrase in their actual handwriting and it was stitched exactly as-is. Reference: "Gonzalo" on a bucket hat, "Alice" on a tote, "Paris" on a collar, "C'EST LA FAUTE" on a cuff hem.
- **What it says:** A first name, two initials side by side (CG, AF), a one-word place ("Paris", "Sonoma"), a short witty French or English phrase ("c'est la faute", "bon", "toujours"). Never a full sentence alone. Never a slogan.
- **Scale:** Small to medium. Never dominates the garment — it's a discovery, not a statement.

### Illustration Motifs
- **Style:** Open outline line drawing — not filled satin stitch blocks. Think: a clean pen sketch translated to thread. Simple, gestural, recognizable at a glance.
- **The motif library (drawn from brand sketches):** Croissant, wine glass, wine bottle, oysters + baguette, lemon branch, bicycle, bistro table and chairs, wisteria hanging from an arch, olive tree in a pot, a picnic basket, a small crab, a sun, a swallow in flight, a red carnation, a four-leaf clover, a small heart, a tiny tulip.
- **Quantity:** 1–3 motifs per piece, scattered loosely. Never symmetrical, never centered like a pattern.
- **Colors:** Muted but specific — dusty red, cornflower blue, warm gold/ochre, olive green, soft pink, chocolate brown. Colors are intentional per motif (red crab, gold sun, blue swallow), never a monochrome set.

### Placement
- **Totes:** Name or initials in cursive + 2–3 scattered motifs across the front face. Motifs placed organically, not in a grid.
- **Apparel (shirts, blouses):** Collar point or cuff edge for a single word or phrase; scattered small motifs across the chest or back; text along a button placket.
- **Hats:** Name in cursive on the front panel or brim; one or two motifs nearby.
- **Accessories (keychains, patches):** Single initial in fine cursive on a patterned or solid oval — gingham, stripe, solid linen backgrounds.

### What It Is Not
- Not a large centered block monogram
- Not a satin-stitch filled letter
- Not a matching symmetrical pattern
- Not a corporate logo or gift-shop initial
- Not thick or heavy — the stitching is fine and precise

## Product Integration Rule
In lifestyle images, the product is **part of the scene, not the subject of the scene.** It should feel discovered, not displayed. A bag is on a chair in the background, slightly out of focus. A napkin is beside a vase. The person carrying the tote has forgotten they are holding it. The embroidery detail is not visible in lifestyle shots — it is only visible in dedicated close-ups.

Only in macro/close-up shots is the product the deliberate focus. Everything else: the product is ambient.

If an image looks like an ad, it is wrong. It should look like a photograph someone took of their life.

## Human Element
- Anonymous and integrated. Hands with rings, a wrist, feet in sandals or barefoot, someone mid-stride, a partial torso. No full faces — the viewer inserts themselves.
- Wardrobe: worn denim, white linen button-down, relaxed cream knit, clean white tee, stone-colored wide-leg trousers. Effortless, not styled.
- Skin: sun-kissed, natural, real texture. Warm and healthy — not pale porcelain, not overly bronzed.
- Energy: mid-motion, unhurried. Carrying flowers home, pouring wine, walking to the car. Life in progress.
